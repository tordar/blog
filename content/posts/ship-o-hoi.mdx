I cancelled Spotify Premium last year after 13 years. My setup isn't necessarily better — but it's mine. I own my listening data, I know where my money goes, and for the first time I have a complete picture of everything I've listened to since 2012.

## The data rabbit hole

It started with a side project. I wanted to visualise my full Spotify listening history — not just the year-end Wrapped summary, but everything, going back to when I first signed up. So I built [Spotify Pulse](https://github.com/tordar/spotify-pulse): a dashboard that processes your exported listening history into stats, charts, genre graphs, and a heatmap of every day you've ever listened.

Getting the data out of Spotify was its own challenge. Their GDPR export is the legal minimum they can get away with — duplicate entries, inconsistent track IDs, different versions of the same song treated as separate plays. It took a significant amount of cleaning to get it into a usable state.

But once it was clean, I had something complete. Every track, every artist, every album, going back over a decade.

That's when the frustration set in. Spotify Wrapped — the one moment a year they let you see your own data — is one of their most valuable marketing assets. [Wrapped pulled over 200 million engaged users in the first 24 hours of its 2025 launch](https://techcrunch.com/2025/12/04/spotify-says-wrapped-2025-is-its-biggest-yet-with-200m-users-in-its-first-day/). They guard your listening history precisely because it's worth something. To them.

## The math didn't add up

I was paying 139 kr a month. That's roughly 1,668 kr a year — for over a decade.

Here's where it goes: Spotify takes around 30% off the top. The remaining 70% goes into a royalty pool distributed not based on what *you* listen to, but proportionally across all streams on the platform. That means the artists you actually care about — the indie acts, the smaller labels — get a fraction of a fraction. The bulk of the payout flows to the major labels, whose artists dominate total stream counts.

Bon Iver — one of the most critically acclaimed artists of the last 15 years — has spoken publicly about how little he receives from streaming. [The numbers are genuinely shocking](https://thetrichordist.com/2020/03/05/2019-2020-streaming-price-bible-youtube-is-still-the-1-problem-to-solve/) when you work them out per stream.

Spotify's CEO [Daniel Ek](https://www.digitalmusicnews.com/2020/08/04/spotify-ceo-controversial-comments/) has made statements over the years that have not endeared him to the artist community — including suggesting that artists need to "release more content" to make streaming economics work for them.

I'm not saying I had a clean solution. But I stopped feeling good about where my money was going.

## Owning your listening history

One of the invisible costs of leaving Spotify is the fear of losing your history. Wrapped is bad, but at least it's something. What happens to 13 years of data if you walk away?

The answer is [ListenBrainz](https://listenbrainz.org) — an open source listening tracker run by the MetaBrainz Foundation. You can import your full Spotify history, then continue tracking going forward from any source. Your scrobbles are yours, stored in a system you can query, export, and own indefinitely.

Once I had my history in ListenBrainz and my Spotify Pulse data cleaned up, I realised something: I could merge all of it — Spotify history, Navidrome library plays, everything — into a single continuous record. The switch didn't have to mean starting over. It meant continuing, just on different infrastructure.
