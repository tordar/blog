## The math didn't add up

I was paying 139 kr a month. That's roughly 1,668 kr a year — for over a decade.

Here's where it goes: Spotify takes around 30% off the top. The remaining 70% goes into a royalty pool distributed not based on what *you* listen to, but proportionally across all streams on the platform. That means the artists you actually care about — the indie acts, the smaller labels — get a fraction of a fraction. The bulk of the payout flows to the major labels, whose artists dominate total stream counts.

Bon Iver — one of the most critically acclaimed artists of the last 15 years — has spoken publicly about how little he receives from streaming. [The numbers are genuinely shocking](LINK_TO_BON_IVER_OR_ARTIST_PAY_PIECE) when you work them out per stream.

Spotify's CEO [Daniel Ek](LINK_TO_EK_CONTROVERSY) has made statements over the years that have not endeared him to the artist community — including suggesting that artists need to "release more content" to make streaming economics work for them.

I'm not saying I had a clean solution. But I stopped feeling good about where my money was going.

## Owning your listening history

One of the invisible costs of leaving Spotify is the fear of losing your history. Wrapped is bad, but at least it's something. What happens to 13 years of data if you walk away?

The answer is [ListenBrainz](https://listenbrainz.org) — an open source listening tracker run by the MetaBrainz Foundation. You can import your full Spotify history, then continue tracking going forward from any source. Your scrobbles are yours, stored in a system you can query, export, and own indefinitely.

Once I had my history in ListenBrainz and my Spotify Pulse data cleaned up, I realised something: I could merge all of it — Spotify history, Navidrome library plays, everything — into a single continuous record. The switch didn't have to mean starting over. It meant continuing, just on different infrastructure.

## Ship O'Hoi 🏴‍☠️

The biggest reason people don't leave Spotify is that they have no music library. You can't just cancel and expect to have something to listen to. Ripping old CDs is the advice you'll find online — it's realistic if you have 20 albums, completely unrealistic if you want anything close to what Spotify offers. It keeps people subscribed.

My solution was [Soulseek](https://www.slsknet.org) — a peer-to-peer file sharing network that's been around since 2000 and has never really gone away. It's not a torrent tracker. It's a direct connection between users sharing their music libraries. The quality is typically high, the catalogue is deep, and the community of people sharing is genuinely dedicated.

Here's the part that made it feel defensible: I used my Spotify Pulse data — the cleaned, complete history of everything I'd ever listened to across 13 years — to generate a list of every artist and album I'd actually played. Then I downloaded it. Music I had already paid for, repeatedly, for over a decade. I wasn't building a pirate library. I was reconstructing one that was already mine.

The practical setup: I built a small web wrapper around the Soulseek command-line binary, hosted on my NAS, accessible via VPN. I can queue a download from anywhere — a new album I've heard about, something I want to revisit — and it lands in my Navidrome library within minutes.
```

## The server

The stack is straightforward. [Navidrome](https://www.navidrome.org) runs on my NAS — a lightweight, open source music server that streams to any device via any Subsonic-compatible client. It handles albums, artists, playlists, and recently played with no fuss.

Metadata is handled by [beets](https://beets.io) with the [MusicBrainz](https://musicbrainz.org) backend. This is worth calling out: bad metadata was the defining frustration of pre-streaming piracy. Wrong album art, chaotic filenames, missing track numbers. It's why people moved to iTunes, and eventually Spotify — centralised services that handled all of that for you.

That problem is largely solved now. beets + MusicBrainz automatically matches your files against a massive, community-maintained database and applies clean, consistent metadata across your library. The tooling has genuinely caught up.

The full setup:
- **Navidrome** — music server, streaming, library management
- **beets + MusicBrainz** — metadata tagging
- **Soulseek wrapper** — download new music directly to the server via VPN
```
